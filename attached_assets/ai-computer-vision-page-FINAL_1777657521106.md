## How AI Computer Vision Works: The Pipeline `[LT: how computer vision works]`

**`[ORIGINAL]` The AGIX Vision Pipeline — from raw pixels to automated decisions:`**

### Stage 1: Image / Video Capture
Cameras, sensors, video streams, uploaded images, scanned documents. Industrial cameras, CCTV, smartphone cameras, medical imaging devices, drone feeds.

### Stage 2: Preprocessing
Resizing, normalization, noise filtering, color correction, augmentation. Ensures consistent input quality regardless of environmental variation.

### Stage 3: Feature Extraction
Deep learning models identify edges, shapes, textures, objects, and spatial relationships. Convolutional neural networks extract hierarchical visual features from raw pixel data.

### Stage 4: Model Inference `[SK: object detection ai]`
Trained models classify, detect, segment, or track based on extracted features. Real-time or batch processing depending on latency requirements. `[TOOL]` YOLOv8/v9 for real-time detection. `[TOOL]` TensorFlow / `[TOOL]` PyTorch for custom models.

### Stage 5: Decision Layer (Most Important) `[ORIGINAL]`
Outputs trigger alerts, actions, and workflows. Defect → reject product. Anomaly → alert security. Document → extract data to CRM. **Without this step, computer vision = passive analytics.**

## ⚠️ Limitations of Computer Vision `[LT: computer vision limitations]`

**[TRUST BUILDER]**

**1. Performance depends heavily on data quality.** Models trained on clean lab images degrade in real-world conditions with variable lighting, camera angles, and environmental noise.

**2. Requires labeled datasets.** Supervised models need annotated training data. Annotation is expensive and domain-specific. AGIX uses active learning and synthetic data to reduce labeling burden.

**3. Struggles with edge cases.** Occlusion, unusual lighting, novel objects, and domain shift cause accuracy drops. Continuous retraining and hard-example mining are essential.

**4. Models need ongoing updates.** Production environments change — new products, new defect types, camera drift. Systems without retraining pipelines decay silently.

**`[ORIGINAL]` Computer vision does not "see like humans." It learns patterns from data. The quality of the data, the rigor of evaluation, and the robustness of deployment determine whether a system works in production — not just in a demo.`**

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is AI computer vision? `[LT]`
AI computer vision `[PK]` enables machines to interpret visual data — images, video, documents — and automate decisions based on what is detected. It uses deep learning to identify objects, defects, text, faces, and patterns at speeds and accuracy levels beyond human capability.

### How does computer vision work? `[LT]`
A computer vision system captures visual input, preprocesses it, extracts features using deep learning, runs model inference (detection, classification, segmentation), and triggers actions based on results. AGIX adds re-ranking and decision layers to convert detection into business operations.

### What is the difference between computer vision and image recognition?
Image recognition classifies entire images into categories ("this is a dog"). Computer vision `[PK]` is the broader field that includes detection (where is the dog?), segmentation (which pixels are the dog?), tracking (where is the dog moving?), and OCR (what text is in the image?).

### What industries use computer vision most?
Manufacturing (quality inspection), healthcare (medical imaging), retail (shelf monitoring), logistics (warehouse automation), security (surveillance), agriculture (crop monitoring), and automotive (autonomous driving). `[CITE]` Manufacturing holds ~27% market share (Roots Analysis).

### Can computer vision work in real-time?
Yes. AGIX deploys real-time systems using `[TOOL]` YOLOv8/v9 for inference and `[TOOL]` NVIDIA Jetson for edge processing. Sub-100ms inference enables real-time production line inspection at 500+ items/minute.

### What is edge deployment?
Edge deployment runs AI models on local hardware (near the cameras) rather than sending data to the cloud. This reduces latency, ensures real-time performance, and keeps sensitive data on-site. `[CITE]` Edge inference represents 66.3% of the AI computer vision market (Coherent Market Insights, 2026).

### How much does a computer vision system cost? `[LT]`
$30K–$55K focused. $55K–$110K production. $110K–$220K+ enterprise/research-grade. Cost driven by dataset size, labeling, environmental variability, and real-time requirements.

### What are the limitations of computer vision? `[LT]`
Performance depends on data quality. Requires labeled datasets. Struggles with edge cases (occlusion, unusual lighting). Models need ongoing retraining as conditions change. Computer vision learns patterns from data — it doesn't "see like humans."

### Can computer vision read documents (OCR)? `[SK: ocr ai]`
Yes. AGIX builds OCR systems using `[TOOL]` PaddleOCR for structured documents and `[TOOL]` GPT-4o Vision for complex, handwritten, or multi-language documents. Extracted data routes directly to CRM, ERP, or automation workflows.

### Can computer vision work with existing cameras?
In many cases, yes. AGIX evaluates existing camera infrastructure during the assessment phase. Resolution, frame rate, lighting, and positioning determine whether existing hardware is sufficient or upgrades are needed.

### How long does it take to build?
Focused: 8–12 weeks. Production: 10–16 weeks. Enterprise: 14–24 weeks. Includes data collection, annotation, model training, evaluation, deployment, and monitoring setup.
